# Decision Theory

## Information

Decision theory is a multidisciplinary field within behavioral science that investigates how individuals and organizations make choices, particularly in situations characterized by uncertainty or complexity. It draws insights from psychology, economics, statistics, and neuroscience to understand and model decision-making processes. At its core, decision theory is concerned with the reasoning underlying an agent's choices, ranging from everyday decisions to significant strategic choices.

The field is broadly divided into two primary branches:

*   **Normative Decision Theory:** This branch prescribes how decisions *should* ideally be made to maximize rationality and utility. It often employs mathematical models, such as expected utility theory, to define optimal choices under assumptions of perfect information and rational actors. The objective is to model an ideal decision process.
*   **Descriptive Decision Theory:** In contrast, descriptive theories aim to observe and explain how people *actually* make decisions in real-world scenarios. This branch accounts for cognitive biases, heuristics, emotions, and psychological factors that often lead individuals to deviate from purely rational models. Behavioral economists like Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky have significantly contributed to this area with theories such as Prospect Theory, which highlights phenomena like loss aversion and framing effects.
*   A third, less commonly cited, branch is **Prescriptive Decision Theory**, which falls between normative and descriptive, using data to suggest the best choices for users.

Key elements and concepts fundamental to decision theory include:

*   **Decision Maker (Agent):** The individual or entity making the choice.
*   **Acts (Alternatives/Courses of Action):** The different choices or strategies available to the decision maker.
*   **Events (States of Nature):** Future occurrences or circumstances beyond the decision maker's control that influence the outcome of a decision.
*   **Outcomes:** The results that arise from a specific combination of an act and an event.
*   **Payoffs:** The numerical values (positive or negative) representing the desirability or value a decision maker places on each outcome. These are often organized in a payoff matrix.
*   **Utility:** The level of satisfaction, value, or benefit an individual derives from a particular outcome. Decision theory assumes individuals have preferences and values that guide their choices, often represented by utility functions.
*   **Risk:** Refers to decision-making conditions where the probabilities of different outcomes are known.
*   **Uncertainty:** Describes situations where outcomes are not fully predictable, and the probabilities of various outcomes are unknown or undefined. Decision theory explicitly addresses the challenges of making choices under uncertainty, where individuals may resort to heuristics or subjective judgments.
*   **Expected Utility Theory:** A foundational model in normative decision-making, it posits that rational agents should make choices that maximize their expected utility by weighing the likelihood of various outcomes against their respective benefits.
*   **Bounded Rationality:** Proposed by Herbert Simon, this concept suggests that decision-makers are limited by their cognitive resources, such as time and intelligence, which can constrain their ability to make perfectly rational decisions.

Decision theory categorizes decisions based on the level of information available:

*   **Deterministic Decisions (Certainty):** Outcomes are known with absolute certainty.
*   **Risky Decisions:** Outcomes are uncertain, but the probabilities of these outcomes are known.
*   **Uncertain Decisions:** Outcomes are completely unknown, and their probabilities cannot be determined.

Applications of decision theory are extensive and span various fields, including:

*   **Economics and Policy-Making:** Used to predict market behaviors, understand consumer choices, and evaluate the potential outcomes and trade-offs of different economic policies.
*   **Business and Finance:** Applied in strategic decisions such as product pricing, resource allocation, investment choices, and understanding market trends.
*   **Healthcare and Medicine:** Utilized in designing and analyzing clinical trials, evaluating environmental impact assessments, and making informed decisions about treatment strategies.
*   **Artificial Intelligence and Algorithm Design:** Helps in designing algorithms that can "think" and make choices under uncertainty.
*   **Psychology:** Essential for studying human decision-making and behavior, including how preferences and risk aversion influence choices.

Common tools and methods in decision theory include decision trees, payoff matrices, and criteria for decision-making under risk or uncertainty such as Expected Monetary Value (EMV), Maximax, Maximin, and Minimax Regret.
