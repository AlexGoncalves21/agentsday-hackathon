# Game Theory

## Information

Game theory studies strategic interaction: situations where the outcome for each participant depends not only on their own decisions but also on the decisions of others. It provides mathematical and conceptual tools for reasoning about conflict, cooperation, incentives, bargaining, coordination, competition, and commitment.

A game usually includes players, available strategies, information, preferences, and payoffs. Games can be simultaneous or sequential, cooperative or non-cooperative, zero-sum or non-zero-sum, finite or repeated, symmetric or asymmetric, and complete-information or incomplete-information. These distinctions matter because different structures produce different kinds of rational behavior.

Nash equilibrium is one of the central concepts. In a Nash equilibrium, no player can improve their outcome by unilaterally changing strategy while the other players keep theirs fixed. This does not mean the outcome is morally good, socially optimal, or collectively efficient. The Prisoner's Dilemma shows how individually rational choices can create collectively worse outcomes. Coordination games show how multiple stable equilibria can exist and why expectations, conventions, signaling, and communication matter.

Game theory is used in economics, political science, military strategy, evolutionary biology, computer science, auctions, mechanism design, cybersecurity, negotiation, and AI. In artificial intelligence, it is useful for multi-agent systems, adversarial settings, tool markets, alignment problems, delegation, resource allocation, and cooperation between agents.

Game theory differs from decision theory because game theory models other decision-makers as part of the environment. Decision theory asks what one agent should do under uncertainty. Game theory asks what happens when multiple agents reason and act in relation to one another.

Important concepts to preserve:

- Nash equilibrium
- Dominant strategy
- Prisoner's Dilemma
- Coordination game
- Zero-sum and non-zero-sum games
- Repeated games
- Signaling
- Mechanism design
- Bargaining
- Mixed strategies
- Payoff matrices

The system needs a structure where their interaction produces better wiki quality rather than local optimization or circular critique.
