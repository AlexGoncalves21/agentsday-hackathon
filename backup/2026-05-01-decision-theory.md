# Decision Theory

## Information

Decision theory studies how agents make, or should make, choices among alternatives under uncertainty. It sits close to economics, philosophy, statistics, psychology, computer science, and AI. It is especially important for understanding agency because an agent is not merely something that knows things; it is something that chooses actions.

Normative decision theory asks what a rational agent ought to do. Descriptive decision theory studies how humans actually make decisions, including biases and deviations from ideal rationality. Prescriptive decision theory tries to improve real-world decisions by giving usable methods and frameworks.

Core concepts include options, states of the world, outcomes, preferences, utility, probability, uncertainty, expected utility, risk, value of information, and dominance. Expected utility theory evaluates actions by combining the desirability of outcomes with their probabilities. This creates a framework for comparing uncertain choices, though it depends heavily on how utilities and probabilities are modeled.

Different decision theories disagree about what counts as the consequence of an action. Causal decision theory evaluates what an action causes. Evidential decision theory evaluates what an action is evidence of. Functional decision theory reasons about the output of a decision procedure and is often discussed in relation to Newcomb-like problems.

Classic puzzles and challenges:

- Newcomb's problem
- Allais paradox
- Ellsberg paradox
- Pascal's wager and Pascal-style reasoning
- St. Petersburg paradox
- Risk aversion
- Bounded rationality
- Dynamic inconsistency

For AI agents, decision theory matters because tool-using systems must choose actions under incomplete information. An agent may need to decide whether to search, edit, ask a question, run tests, spawn a sub-agent, stop, or continue. Good agent architecture should encode decision points and exit criteria rather than letting the model improvise forever.

Decision theory differs from game theory because game theory includes other strategic agents. Decision theory can model a single agent facing uncertainty. Game theory models interdependent choices between multiple agents.
